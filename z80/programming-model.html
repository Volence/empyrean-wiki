Z80 Assembly

The Programming Model

This is the Genesis's second CPU, the small one that usually runs the sound driver, and none of the 68000 is required to follow this page. Where a comparison to the 68000 helps, this page draws it.

1 · The accumulator and the 8-bit registers

A Z80 program does almost all of its work in a handful of tiny storage slots built into the processor, called registers. There are seven you compute with, and every one of them holds exactly 8 bits, a single byte. Their names are short: A, B, C, D, E, H, and L. An eighth register, F, is special: it holds status flags rather than a value you work with, and Section 3 is entirely about it. You name a register to use it (A, B), the way you would name a variable. Unlike a byte of memory, a register has no address of its own.

One of the seven is not like the others. A is the accumulator, the register the Z80's 8-bit arithmetic-and-logic unit (the part of the chip that adds, subtracts, and compares) is physically wired to. When you add, subtract, AND, OR, XOR, or compare one byte against another, the CPU reads one operand from A and does the work. For the 8-bit forms of ADD A,r, ADC A,r, SUB, SBC A,r, AND r, OR r, and XOR r, the answer is written straight back into A, and the flags in F report on how it came out. Compare, CP r, is the odd one out: it subtracts to set the flags exactly as SUB would, but throws the result away, so it tells you how A and the other value relate without changing A at all. (The 16-bit ADC HL,rr and SBC HL,rr are a separate story, since those target HL, not A.) B through L are the general-purpose supporting cast: they hold the other values, hand them to A to be worked on, and hold results afterward.

It would make a tidy story to say every calculation flows through A, but that overstates it. A is the accumulator for 8-bit arithmetic, logic, and comparison, which is a genuinely large share of the everyday work, yet plenty of operations bypass it entirely. INC and DEC (add or subtract one) act on any 8-bit register directly. The bit operations (BIT, SET, RES) and the rotates and shifts also work on any register, or on the byte in memory at (HL). And the 16-bit additions, which Section 2 covers, add register pairs together and never touch A. So the accurate framing is this: A is where 8-bit math and logic happen, while many single-register operations, and all the 16-bit ones, go around it.

New here? · What does "accumulator" mean?

Picture an old desktop calculator with a single running total on its display. You do not get to compute "3 plus 4" in the abstract; you press a number, then press +, then press another number, and the total on the display updates. Every operation reads that one display and writes its answer back to it. That display is an accumulator: the one place results pile up, or accumulate.

On the Z80, A is that display. The chip has one 8-bit adder-and-comparer, and it is wired so that one side of it is always A. To add two bytes you get one of them into A first, then add the second; the sum lands in A, ready for the next step. The other registers (B through L) are like scraps of paper where you park numbers you will feed into the calculator later. This is why so much Z80 code has the rhythm "load a value into A, do something to it, read the result out of A."

If you have seen the 68000, the contrast is the quickest way in, though you need none of it here. That chip gives you eight equal, interchangeable 32-bit data registers, d0 through d7; you can add into any one of them just as easily as any other. The Z80 takes the opposite approach: one privileged 8-bit accumulator plus six general 8-bit registers that feed it. Different philosophy, same underlying job of holding the values a program is working on. (The 68000 Programming Model page covers that side if you are curious.)

Here is the accumulator doing an ordinary 8-bit job, lifted straight from Aeon's Z80 sound driver as it polls the FM sound chip:

        ld      a, (SND_Z80_YM_A0)       ; YM status: bit0 = Timer A overflow
        and     SND_TIMERA_OVF_MASK
engine/sound/z80_sound_driver.asm:363

The first line loads the FM chip's status byte into A. The second ANDs that byte with a mask that has just one bit set, the "Timer A overflowed" bit. AND keeps a bit only where both inputs have it, so afterward A holds that single bit and nothing else: non-zero if the timer overflowed, zero if it did not. Notice that both the read value and the result live in A, because that is exactly where 8-bit logic like AND operates. The instruction names only the mask (SND_TIMERA_OVF_MASK is a named constant, not a register); A is the implied other operand and the destination, never written in the line. (The driver then branches on whether A came out zero, which is Section 3's flags at work.)

Teaching example · not engine code

The same shape with plain numbers, to see the accumulator fill and update:

        ld      a, 5     ; A = 5
        add     a, 3     ; A = 8   (A + 3, answer back into A)

There is no add b, 3 that behaves this way: 8-bit addition is defined on A. To add 3 to B, you would move B into A, add, and move it back. (You could also INC three times to reach the same value, but that is not the same as adding 3 where the flags are concerned: INC leaves the carry C untouched, and after three of them H and P/V describe only the final increment.)

2 · Register pairs

Two of the 8-bit registers can be glued together and used as a single 16-bit register. Join B and C and you have BC; the same trick gives DE (from D and E) and HL (from H and L). This joined-up form is called a register pair, and much of the Z80's character comes from it.

In each pair the first-named register holds the high byte and the second holds the low byte. So B is the high byte of BC and C is the low byte; D is high and E low in DE; H is high and L low in HL. Read as one number, BC with B = $19 and C = $2A is the 16-bit value $192A: the high byte supplies the top two hex digits, the low byte the bottom two.

Why bother joining them? A single 8-bit register only counts from 0 to 255, nowhere near enough to name a location in the Z80's memory; a memory address needs a full 16 bits. A register pair is exactly 16 bits wide, so a pair can hold an address. That is what turns the pairs into the Z80's pointers, the registers you load with an address and then reach memory through.

HL is the one built for that job. Written in parentheses, (HL) means "the byte in memory at the address currently in HL." Many 8-bit load, arithmetic, logic, shift, and bit instructions accept (HL) as a memory operand in the same places they accept an 8-bit register: instead of naming a register you name (HL), and the instruction works on the byte HL points at. It is not literally every position (there is no ld (hl),(hl), for instance), but the pattern is broad enough that (HL) is the Z80's everyday way to reach memory, which makes HL its general-purpose pointer.

New here? · What does (HL) mean?

Think of the number in HL as an address written on an envelope, not the letter inside. Name the pair plainly, HL, and you are talking about that 16-bit number itself. Wrap it in parentheses, (HL), and you are talking about the byte that lives at that address in memory. It is the difference between a street address on an envelope and the house standing at that address.

So a pair like HL does not store your data; it stores where your data is. To read the byte, the CPU takes the address out of HL, goes to that spot in memory, and brings the byte back. Following an address to the thing it names has a name of its own: dereferencing. The parentheses are the Z80's way of writing it.

The other pairs can reach memory too, but on a much shorter leash. In ordinary explicit byte loads, BC and DE address memory in just four forms: LD A,(BC) and LD A,(DE) to load a byte, and LD (BC),A and LD (DE),A to store one. Only A can be the other side, and you cannot do arithmetic on the address in the same breath. (DE also serves as an implied destination pointer in the block-move instructions such as LDIR, but that is a specialized use for a later page.) IX and IY, two more 16-bit registers, reach memory a third way, by indexed addressing such as (IX+d) with a signed displacement, which Section 4 covers. So the pairs are not interchangeable pointers: HL is the flexible general pointer, BC and DE are A-only address registers, and IX/IY are the displacement-indexed pair.

If the 68000 is your reference point, HL is the closest thing the Z80 has to one of its address registers, but the resemblance is loose: the 68000's a0 through a6 are interchangeable pointers (a7 doubles as its stack pointer), whereas the Z80's pairs each carry their own rules. (Optional reading; the 68000 Programming Model page has that side.)

Here is a pair being loaded in one stroke, from Aeon's sound driver as it sets up DAC streaming:

        ld      de, SND_Z80_YM_A1        ; de = $4001 (DAC data port; held all streaming)
engine/sound/z80_sound_driver.asm:341

A single instruction drops the full 16-bit constant $4001 into DE, filling both halves at once (D becomes $40, E becomes $01). The driver parks that address in DE and streams every sample byte out through it.

And here is HL working as a pointer. A few lines earlier the driver set H to a fixed value, $19; now it sets L and reads through the pair:

        ld      l, c                     ; l = RD (h = $19)
        ld      a, (hl)                  ; ring[rd]
engine/sound/z80_sound_driver.asm:356

H is holding the page $19 and L is set to an index (c, the ring read position), so together HL points at ring[rd], one byte in a small buffer. ld a,(hl) then reads that byte into A. The same two registers are doing double duty here: individually as the bytes H and L that you set one at a time, and jointly as the single pointer HL that names a memory address.

3 · The flag register F, and AF

Every time the Z80 does a piece of arithmetic or logic, it leaves a few little yes/no notes behind about how the result came out: was it zero, did it go negative, did it overflow. Those notes live in the eighth register, F, and a later instruction can read them to decide what to do next.

F is the flag register, the Z80's status register. Unlike A through L, you do not load a value into F and compute with it; instead each of its bits is a separate flag, a single yes/no fact set automatically by the last operation. Section 1 called F "special" and pointed here; this is the whole of it.

A and F can be treated as the register pair AF, with A as the high byte and F as the low byte. Watch out here, though, because this is not a general 16-bit working register the way Section 2's BC, DE, and HL are: there is no ld af,$1234, no inc af, no add hl,af. AF exists for one narrow but important job, moving the accumulator and its flags together in a single step. The accumulator holds the result you just computed and F holds the flags describing it, so it is natural to save or swap the two as a unit; the instruction push af stows both at once (you will meet the stack it pushes to in Section 4), and Section 5's ex af,af' exchanges them as a unit too.

New here? · What is a flag, and who reads it?

A flag is one bit that answers one yes/no question about the last thing the CPU did. Picture a row of small mailbox flags, each either up or down. After the Z80 subtracts two numbers, it flips the "was the answer zero?" flag up if they were equal, and down if they were not. It does this for you, every time, without being asked.

The reader is the next instruction. Z80 code almost never tests a value directly; instead it does a calculation, which sets the flags, and then a conditional jump looks at one flag and branches on it. jp z,somewhere means "jump to somewhere only if the zero flag is up"; jr c,elsewhere means "jump only if the carry flag is up." So the rhythm of Z80 logic is two beats: an operation that quietly sets flags, then a jump that reads one. The flags are the wire between "what just happened" and "what to do about it."

Here is the exact layout of F, bit 7 down to bit 0. This table is the reference to come back to; the notes below it explain the ones that need care.

BitNameMeaning
7S (Sign)A copy of the result's top bit. When a value is read as signed, S = 1 means the result is negative.
6Z (Zero)Set to 1 when the result is zero. The most-tested flag by far.
5unusedUndocumented; not a flag you set out to use.
4H (Half-carry)Set when a carry or borrow crosses between bit 3 and bit 4 for 8-bit arithmetic (for a 16-bit add it is the carry between bit 11 and bit 12). The Z80 uses it to fix up binary-coded-decimal arithmetic. Not directly testable by a branch.
3unusedUndocumented; not a flag you set out to use.
2P/V (Parity/Overflow)Dual-purpose. After logic operations it reports parity; after arithmetic it reports signed overflow. See the note below.
1N (Add/Subtract)0 after an add, 1 after a subtract. The Z80 uses it, together with H, to correct binary-coded-decimal results. Not directly testable by a branch.
0C (Carry)The carry out of an add, or the borrow from a subtract. The other heavily-tested flag.

Four of these flags you branch on directly. Z (zero) and C (carry) are the workhorses: nearly every loop and comparison ends with a jump that reads one of them. S (sign) is simply a copy of the result's top bit, which is the sign bit when a byte is read as a signed number; a set S means negative. P/V you can also branch on, but its meaning depends on what set it, which the next note untangles.

The other two, half-carry (H) and add/subtract (N), are a matched pair the Z80 keeps for one specific job: correcting binary-coded-decimal arithmetic (packing a decimal digit into each nibble of a byte). The DAA instruction reads both to adjust a result back into valid decimal form. You do not test H or N with a branch; there is no conditional jump that looks at them. They exist for DAA's benefit, and it is fair to mostly ignore them until you do decimal math. Bits 5 and 3 carry no defined meaning at all; they are undocumented and not flags you use.

One rule underlies all of this and is worth holding onto: each instruction decides for itself which flags it updates and which it leaves untouched. Not every operation rewrites all six. Many arithmetic and logic instructions on A update the whole set, but others move only some, and a few move none at all: inc hl and dec hl (and the other 16-bit increments) change no flags whatsoever, and add hl,rr updates H, N, and C while leaving S, Z, and P/V exactly as an earlier instruction left them. That is not an oversight, it is useful: it lets code set a flag with one instruction and branch on it several instructions later. When you reach the instruction pages, each entry spells out precisely which flags it moves.

New here? · Why is one bit doing two jobs?

P/V is the one flag whose meaning changes with the instruction that set it, which is confusing until you see why. The Z80 had exactly one spare flag bit and two different useful facts to report, so it made the bit report whichever one fits the operation.

After a logic operation (AND, OR, XOR), the bit is parity: it is 1 when the number of 1-bits in the result is even, 0 when odd. Parity was handy for the serial-communication code the Z80 was designed for. After an arithmetic operation (add, subtract, compare), the same bit is instead overflow: it is set when a signed result slips outside the range a signed byte can hold, which is -128 to +127. Adding two positive numbers and landing on a "negative" result is the classic overflow the flag catches.

So the honest one-line description is "parity after logic, overflow after arithmetic," never just one of the two. (It also carries a couple of special-case meanings, such as reflecting the interrupt state after LD A,I, but the parity/overflow split is the part you use.)

Here is the two-beat rhythm from the explainer in real driver code. Section 1 read the FM chip's status byte into A and masked it; the very next line is the jump that reads the flag that masking left behind:

        ld      a, (SND_Z80_YM_A0)       ; YM status: bit0 = Timer A overflow
        and     SND_TIMERA_OVF_MASK
        jp      nz, SndDrv_TimerATick    ; overflow -> tick (spill/frame/refill/reload), rejoin
engine/sound/z80_sound_driver.asm:363

The and leaves its result in A and, as a side effect, sets the zero flag: Z = 1 if the masked byte came out zero (timer had not overflowed), Z = 0 if a bit survived (it had). The driver never compares A to anything; it just reads the flag. jp nz means "jump if not zero," so control leaves for SndDrv_TimerATick exactly when the overflow bit was set, and falls through otherwise. The and set the note; the jp nz read it. That is the whole loop of Z80 decision-making, and it is why the flag register earns a section of its own.

If the 68000 is your reference point, its condition-code register holds X N Z V C, and the Z80's set is a deliberate variation on the same idea. Reading the shared names first: N means different things on the two chips (negative on the 68000, add/subtract on the Z80), while the Z80's sign flag is named S. Z and C match. The Z80 has no X (extend) bit; it adds a half-carry H and an add/subtract N, both purely for decimal-adjust. Overflow is handled differently too: the 68000 has a dedicated overflow bit V and no parity flag at all, while the Z80 has no separate V and instead reuses its one P/V bit for either parity or overflow depending on the instruction. Same principle, a result leaves flags behind for the next instruction, with a different bit budget. (Optional; the 68000 Programming Model page covers that register.)

4 · IX, IY, SP, and PC

Four more registers round out the model, all of them 16 bits wide, and unlike the working registers of Sections 1 to 3 each has a single dedicated job. Two of them, IX and IY, you point at data; the other two, SP and PC, the CPU keeps pointed at housekeeping structures for you. The instructions that use them get their own page later; here we just name each register and what it holds.

IX and IY are the Z80's two index registers. Each holds a 16-bit address, like HL, but their signature move is indexed addressing: (IX+d) means "the byte at the address in IX plus a small signed displacement d." That d is an 8-bit signed value, so it reaches from -128 to +127 bytes from the base. Point an index register at the start of a small data structure and each field is a fixed offset away, so you read or write any field by naming the base once and the offset per access.

Aeon's sound driver does exactly this while it sets up a song's channels. IY points at a header record and IX at the live channel it is filling in; each named offset is one field:

        ld      a, (iy+SHC_ROUTE)
        ld      (ix+sc_route), a
engine/sound/z80_sound_driver.asm:1256

The first line reads the header's route field (SHC_ROUTE is that field's offset from IY) into A; the second stores it into the channel's own route field (sc_route is that field's offset from IX). Both registers stay parked on their structure's base while the offsets pick out fields, which is the whole point of an index register.

SP is the stack pointer. It holds the address of the top of the stack, a scratch area the CPU uses to stow values and, above all, return addresses when one routine calls another. The stack is last-in-first-out: the most recently pushed value is the first one popped back. Two details matter and both are easy to get backwards. First, the stack grows downward in memory: PUSH subtracts 2 from SP and then writes, so each pushed value sits at a lower address than the last, and POP reads and then adds 2 back. Second, you set where the stack lives once, at startup, by loading SP with its top address; the driver does this on its first breath:

        ld      sp, SND_STACK_TOP        ; stack top $1FFE (see the z80-ram-map spec)
engine/sound/z80_sound_driver.asm:143
New here? · What is a stack, and why does it grow downward?

A stack is a pile you can only add to or take from at the top, like a stack of plates: you set a plate on top, and the next plate you lift off is the one you just set down. Computers lean on this because it matches how routines call each other. When routine A calls B, the CPU pushes A's return address; if B then calls C, C's return address goes on top. When C finishes it pops its address (the top plate) and returns to B; when B finishes it pops the next one and returns to A. Last in, first out, so the addresses come back in exactly the right order.

Why downward? It is a convention chosen so the stack and your program's data can share one block of memory without colliding. Put your variables at the low addresses and grow them upward; put the stack at the high addresses and grow it downward; the two march toward each other and you only run out of room when they actually meet. That is why SND_STACK_TOP is set to a high address ($1FFE) and every PUSH steps down from there.

The stack is why Section 3 could promise that push af stows the accumulator and its flags together. The sound driver's interrupt handler leans on it hard: because an interrupt can fire in the middle of anything, the handler pushes every register it will touch on entry and pops them back on exit, so the interrupted code never notices. Watch the order, it is the last-in-first-out rule in the flesh:

        push    af
        push    bc
        push    de
        push    hl
        ; ... handle the interrupt ...
        pop     hl
        pop     de
        pop     bc
        pop     af
engine/sound/z80_sound_driver.asm:489

The pushes go af, bc, de, hl; the pops come back hl, de, bc, af, exact mirror image. hl was pushed last so it is popped first, and every register lands back in the register it left from. Each push stepped SP down; each pop stepped it back up, so SP ends exactly where it began.

PC is the program counter. It holds the address of the instruction the CPU is fetching right now, and it advances on its own as each instruction runs, so straight-line code simply flows from one instruction to the next. You never load PC directly the way you load A or HL; instead the control-flow instructions rewrite it for you. A jump drops a new address into PC; a call does that after first pushing the return address, the address of the instruction right after the call, onto the stack; a return pops that saved address back into PC. Every branch you write in Section 3's two-beat rhythm is, under the hood, a conditional edit to PC.

If the 68000 is your reference point, all four have close cousins there and the comparison is optional. The 68000 also has a program counter and a stack pointer (its address register a7), and its own indexed addressing reaches memory through an address register plus an index. The shapes rhyme; the Z80 just spends fewer, narrower registers on the same jobs. (The 68000 Programming Model page covers that side.)

5 · The shadow register set

Here is the Z80's best "wait, what?" trick: it carries a second, complete copy of its main registers, kept invisibly in the background. There is another AF, another BC, another DE, and another HL, written with a prime mark as AF', BC', DE', and HL'. It is not one all-or-nothing spare set, though: it comes in two halves that swap independently, the BC/DE/HL trio as one bank and AF as the other, so you can have the shadow BC/DE/HL live while the main AF stays put (the sound driver does exactly that below). This is the shadow register set (also called the alternate set), and nothing about it looks like the tidy, one-of-each CPU the earlier sections described.

You move between the two sets with a swap, and there are exactly two swap instructions. EXX swaps the whole trio BC/DE/HL with their primed counterparts BC'/DE'/HL' in one stroke, and pointedly leaves AF alone. EX AF,AF' handles the other half: it swaps AF with AF' and touches nothing else. Between the two you can exchange any part of the set, but always a whole pair at a time, never a single byte.

That "whole pair at a time" is the catch, and it is the thing to get right. The shadow registers are not eight extra registers you can name. There is no ld b',5 and no way to add into HL'; no ordinary instruction reads or writes a primed register directly. The one thing you can do with the shadow set is swap it in. Once EXX has made it live, its BC/DE/HL are simply the ordinary BC/DE/HL that every instruction already knows, and the set you swapped out becomes the hidden one. So the honest description is not "sixteen registers"; it is one register set with a spare, plus a single cheap instruction to trade one for the other.

New here? · Why would a CPU have a second set of registers?

Imagine you are partway through a careful calculation, papers spread across your desk in a particular arrangement, when the phone rings and demands a quick, unrelated task. You have two choices. You could photograph the whole desk, sweep it clear, do the new task, then lay every paper back exactly where it was; or you could pull a second desk alongside, spin your chair to it, do the task, and spin back, your first desk untouched the entire time.

The first way is what Section 4's push/pop dance does: to borrow the registers you save every one to the stack and restore them afterward, several instructions and several memory writes each way. The second way is the shadow set: one EXX spins your chair to a fresh set of registers, you work, one EXX spins back, and the set you left is exactly as you left it. When a task interrupts you often and you must be quick about it, a second desk beats packing and unpacking the first one every single time.

That speed is the whole point, and it is why the shadow set earns its keep in one place above all: a fast context switch. When an interrupt fires, the handler needs some working registers, but it must hand them back untouched or the interrupted code will find its world rearranged. Section 4 showed the careful way, pushing af/bc/de/hl on entry and popping them on exit. The shadow set offers a shortcut: EXX on the way in to grab a private BC/DE/HL, EXX on the way out to give them back, with no stack traffic at all. Sound drivers, which run their mixing on a tight interrupt many times a second, reach for this constantly.

Aeon's sound driver uses it for something a little subtler than a whole context switch: it keeps one running counter in the shadow set so that its main HL stays free for other work. The DAC sample-streaming loop (which runs with interrupts off) walks a small ring buffer with the main HL as its pointer, and it separately needs to count down the number of ROM bytes still to stream. Rather than spend a main register on that counter, it parks the count in the shadow HL. It sets that up once, before the loop begins:

        exx
        ld      hl, (SND_ROM_LEN)        ; hl' = ROM len (shadow set)
        exx
engine/sound/z80_sound_driver.asm:348

Then, each time through the loop, after it has copied one byte from ROM into the ring, it ducks into the shadow set just long enough to tick that counter down and test it, and ducks straight back out:

        exx
        dec     hl                       ; len-- (shadow)
        ld      a, h
        or      l
        exx
engine/sound/z80_sound_driver.asm:382

Read it a line at a time. The first exx makes the shadow set live, so the hl named on the next line is the counter, not the ring pointer. dec hl drops the remaining length by one. The pair ld a,h then or l is a small Z80 idiom worth pocketing: there is no single instruction that tests a 16-bit pair for zero, so you copy the high byte into A and OR the low byte onto it. The result is zero only when both bytes were zero, which leaves the zero flag set exactly when HL has reached $0000. The second exx swaps the main set back in, so the ring pointer in the main HL returns untouched, ready for the next byte. Two swaps bracket the whole thing, and outside them the counter does not exist as far as the rest of the loop can tell. (The jp z that reads the flag this sets sits on the very next line, ending the stream when the count hits zero.)

Aeon's driver never needs EX AF,AF', but it is worth seeing once, because it is the exact piece EXX leaves out. Where EXX saves your working pairs, EX AF,AF' saves the accumulator and, just as importantly, the flags that ride in F alongside it, all in one instruction:

Teaching example · not engine code

An interrupt handler that only needs A and the flags can preserve them with a single swap on the way in and out, instead of a push af / pop af pair:

handler:
        ex      af, af'      ; stash A and flags in the shadow AF
        ; ... do a little work in A, freely changing the flags ...
        ex      af, af'      ; restore A and flags, exactly as they were
        reti

The first swap hides the interrupted code's A and flags in AF'; the second brings them back. Because the flag register rides inside AF, this is a cheap way to borrow the accumulator without disturbing whatever comparison the interrupted code was in the middle of. A maskable interrupt handler conventionally ends with reti rather than plain ret; on the Genesis, which has no Z80 interrupt daisy-chain, a plain ret returns just the same, but reti is the portable habit. (Note that reti does not by itself re-enable interrupts.)

If the 68000 is your reference point, there is simply nothing to compare this to, which is itself the point. The 68000 has no shadow registers; when it needs to free up registers it saves them the long way, writing them out to the stack with MOVEM (move multiple) and reading them back the same way, much like the Z80's own push/pop route from Section 4. The one-instruction bulk swap is a Z80 flavour with no equivalent on the bigger chip, a small piece of cleverness worth savouring. (Optional; the 68000 Programming Model page covers how it saves registers.)

Deep reference · the I and R registers

Two more 8-bit registers exist that a beginner can safely skip; they are here for completeness and for the curious. Neither is a working register you compute in.

I, the Interrupt Page Address register. It holds 8 bits and matters in one specific mode, interrupt mode 2. In that mode, when a device interrupts the CPU, the Z80 builds the address of the handler to run by taking I as the high 8 bits and letting the interrupting device supply the low 8 bits. Together those form a 16-bit address into a table of handler pointers. So I chooses which page of memory that table lives on, and the device chooses the entry within it.

R, the Memory Refresh register. Also 8 bits, and mostly the CPU's own business. Its low 7 bits increment automatically after every instruction fetch; the Z80 was designed to drive dynamic RAM, which forgets its contents unless periodically refreshed, and this counter walks through the rows to keep them alive. Bit 7 is not part of that count: it holds whatever was last written by LD R,A. Because the low bits keep changing on their own, R is sometimes read as a cheap ever-changing value, a weak stand-in for a random number. It is not a real random-number generator (its pattern is entirely predictable), so it is fine for a shimmer effect and wrong for anything that needs true randomness.
